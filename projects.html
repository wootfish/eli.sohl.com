theseus +

I've been working on Theseus since early 2017. The goal: To create a robust system for sharing and preserving research. The project was first conceived as a response to governmental attempts at censorship of climate change research.

The system consists of two parts: The actual application described above - Theseus - and a distributed hash table (DHT) on top of which the application is built.

The DHT is a very simple distributed database that the application uses to form peer-to-peer connections and share data.

In theory, any number of DHT algorithms could be used, but I'm making a new one. The working name for it is Theseus DHT. The motivation for this extra effort is to try and improve on the state of the art for defending against certain categories of attack, particularly Sybil attacks.

The implementation is not yet complete, but the theoretical results achieved so far (the best of which I'm still in the process of writing up) are striking.

There's a lot about Theseus and Theseus DHT on my blog. Here's a list of those posts. Bear in mind that not everything in them is up to date.

cryptopals +

These are a series of cryptography challenges written by the folks at Matasano Security (which I believe has since been absorbed by NCC Group). Sets 1 through 7 are available here, and you can check out Set 8 here. They start out easy enough, but the later sets have a few genuinely tricky problems hidden in them.

You can check out my solutions on Github. I've worked through sets 1 through 7 and plan on taking on set 8 soon.

Back in the day I was very into Project Euler (I think I'm still in the 99.4th percentile or so on there after half a decade of inactivity). I mention that because to me, a lot of what makes Project Euler fun can also be found in Cryptopals. It's a different and more interesting sort of programming, and it's just so satisfying when you get it right.
